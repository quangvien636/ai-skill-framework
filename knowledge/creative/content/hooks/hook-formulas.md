## Summary

Defines honest opening patterns for earning attention by making relevance and
value clear. Retrieve it when drafting the first line, first beat, title, or
opening promise.

## Applies To

- skill:content-creation

## Scope

### Includes

- Problem, outcome, contrast, question, observation, demonstration, and
  specificity-based hook patterns.

### Excludes

- Fabricated urgency, fear manipulation, unsupported outcome claims, and
  platform algorithm tactics.

## Guidance

A hook is a compact contract with the audience: it identifies why the content is
relevant and creates a promise the body can fulfill.

Use these formulas as structures, not fixed phrases:

- Problem and relief: “If [specific friction], try [bounded approach].”
- Outcome and path: “[Credible outcome] starts with [unexpected or useful step].”
- Contrast: “Most advice says [common approach]; use [supported distinction]
  when [condition].”
- Diagnostic question: “Are you seeing [observable symptom] even after [current
  effort]?”
- Specific observation: “[Concrete detail] is usually where [problem] begins.”
- Demonstration: “Here is [process] in [small number] visible steps.”
- Open loop with boundary: “The overlooked part of [topic] is [area]; here is
  what it changes.”

The next line or beat should begin fulfilling the promise immediately. Curiosity
must come from a meaningful information gap, never from withholding the subject
or exaggerating stakes.

## Decision Rules

1. If the brief supplies a clear audience problem, prefer problem-and-relief or
   diagnostic-question framing.
2. If an outcome lacks evidence, frame the hook around a process or possibility,
   not a guarantee.
3. If a contrast cannot be supported, remove the comparison.
4. If the hook could apply unchanged to unrelated topics, add a concrete subject
   detail.
5. If the body does not fulfill the hook, revise the hook or the body before
   delivery.

## Examples

### Good Example

“Your weekly plan may be creating more context switching. Check these three
handoff points before adding another task.” The body then explains exactly three
handoff points.

### Counterexample

“Nobody wants you to know this one insane trick.” The line hides the subject,
manufactures conflict, and promises unsupported novelty.

## Limitations and Risks

- A strong hook cannot repair weak evidence or an irrelevant message.
- Formula repetition can make content predictable; vary the underlying insight,
  not merely punctuation.
- Sensitive topics may require a direct contextual opening rather than
  curiosity.
