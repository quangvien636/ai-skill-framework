# Tone Guidelines

- **Version:** 1.0.0

## Summary

Defines a practical method for translating a requested tone into consistent
language choices. Retrieve it when adapting content to an audience without
copying a named person's distinctive style.

## Applies To

- skill:content-creation

## Scope

### Includes

- Tone dimensions, voice consistency, readability, specificity, and safe style
  adaptation.

### Excludes

- Brand-specific voice systems, translation, accessibility standards, and
  imitation of identifiable living people.

## Guidance

Express tone through observable choices: sentence length, vocabulary,
formality, energy, emotional distance, humor, and directness. Convert vague
labels into two or three compatible dimensions before drafting. For example,
“warm and expert” means plain language, calm confidence, useful specifics, and
no status performance.

Match audience knowledge. Define necessary terms for beginners; for experienced
audiences, remove generic setup and lead with the useful distinction. Maintain
one stable voice across hook, body, and call to action.

Prefer concrete verbs and nouns over inflated adjectives. Use humor only when it
does not obscure the message or target a vulnerable person. A requested style
associated with a living creator must be translated into high-level traits,
such as “fast-paced, playful, and concise,” without copying signature phrases or
distinctive expression.

## Decision Rules

1. If tone descriptors conflict, prioritize audience comprehension and the
   content objective.
2. If a style request names a living person, decline imitation and use neutral
   high-level characteristics.
3. If the audience is unfamiliar with the subject, explain essential terms at
   first use.
4. If humor weakens clarity, trust, or factual precision, remove it.
5. If no tone is supplied, use clear, conversational, respectful language.

## Examples

### Good Example

For “practical and reassuring” content aimed at first-time founders, use short
sentences, acknowledge the difficulty once, define the next step, and avoid
guarantees.

### Counterexample

For “authoritative” content, fill every sentence with jargon, absolute claims,
and invented certainty, or reproduce a named creator's catchphrases.

## Limitations and Risks

- Tone labels are culturally variable and require human review for sensitive
  audiences.
- This guidance does not establish a company's proprietary brand voice.
- Clear prose cannot compensate for an unsupported or misleading claim.
