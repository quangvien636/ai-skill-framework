# Call-to-Action Patterns

- **Knowledge ID:** `kb:creative:content:conversion:call-to-action-patterns`
- **Status:** Active

- **Domain:** `content`
- **Topic:** `conversion`
- **Version:** 1.0.0
- **Last updated:** 2026-07-05

## Summary

Defines proportional call-to-action patterns that connect content value to one
clear next step. Retrieve it when choosing or writing the close of a content
package.

## Applies To

- skill:content-creation

## Scope

### Includes

- Learn, save, discuss, try, compare, subscribe, and conversion-oriented action
  patterns.

### Excludes

- Checkout design, sales funnels, tracking, dark patterns, fabricated scarcity,
  and platform automation.

## Guidance

Use one primary action. It should name a specific behavior, explain enough value
to make the choice intelligible, and require effort proportional to the
audience's trust and awareness.

Useful patterns include:

- Apply: “Try [specific step] when [clear situation].”
- Save: “Save this checklist for [specific future moment].”
- Discuss: “Which of [bounded options] matches your experience, and why?”
- Continue learning: “Read or watch [next resource] for [specific extension].”
- Compare: “Use [criteria] to compare this with your current approach.”
- Subscribe: “Follow for [defined recurring value],” only when that value will
  actually recur.
- Convert: “Start [trial, booking, or purchase] if [fit condition],” with terms
  and limitations left intact.

Do not stack requests such as like, comment, share, save, follow, and buy. Do not
ask for interaction solely to inflate distribution. If the brief specifies a
call to action, preserve its intended outcome while correcting deception,
unsupported urgency, or disproportionate commitment.

## Decision Rules

1. If the audience is early in awareness, prefer learning, saving, or a small
   trial over a high-commitment conversion.
2. If the content teaches a repeatable process, use an apply or save action.
3. If the content presents a genuine decision, use a bounded discussion or
   comparison action.
4. If no action is supplied, choose the smallest useful next step implied by the
   objective.
5. If an action depends on unavailable terms, eligibility, or evidence, flag it
   for human review instead of inventing details.

## Examples

### Good Example

After a weekly-planning video: “Save this and try the 20-minute review on Friday;
then keep the one change that reduces the most handoffs.”

### Counterexample

“Like, comment YES, tag three friends, follow, and buy today before it is too
late,” when no deadline or scarcity was supplied.

## Limitations and Risks

- A suitable call to action does not establish legal compliance for promotions,
  endorsements, subscriptions, or purchases.
- Conversion language requires human review when price, eligibility, claims, or
  terms are material.
- Engagement is not a substitute for audience value.
