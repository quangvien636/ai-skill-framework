## Summary

Defines observable criteria for checking tone against the brief, audience,
subject, and internal consistency.

## Applies To

- skill:review-quality
- workflow:draft-to-reviewed-package

## Scope

### Includes

- Formality, warmth, directness, confidence, vocabulary, sentence movement,
  emotional register, and ethical consistency.

### Excludes

- Imitation of living creators and preference-based rewriting without a brief.

## Guidance

Translate tone labels into observable choices. “Clear and reassuring” may use
plain terms, direct sentences, calm qualification, and specific next steps.
Check whether those choices remain stable across the hook, body, evidence,
limitations, and CTA.

Tone must fit the audience's knowledge, the seriousness of the subject, and the
confidence justified by evidence. A lively opening cannot become deceptive
urgency; an authoritative voice cannot hide uncertainty; a friendly CTA cannot
trivialize safety or risk.

## Decision Rules

1. Cite an observable mismatch, not “the vibe feels wrong.”
2. Prefer local correction when the overall voice is consistent.
3. Treat confidence beyond evidence as an evidence issue as well as tone.
4. Do not recommend imitation of a living person.
5. Preserve necessary warnings and uncertainty even when they interrupt style.

## Examples

### Good Example

A review notes that one absolute sentence conflicts with an otherwise measured,
evidence-limited research brief and proposes calibrated wording.

### Counterexample

A reviewer rewrites the entire draft into their preferred casual voice despite
the brief requesting concise professional language.

## Limitations and Risks

- Tone judgments have cultural and audience dependencies.
- Localization and sensitive audiences may require specialist review.
