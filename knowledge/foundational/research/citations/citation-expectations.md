# Citation Expectations

## Summary

Defines minimum citation identity, locator, consistency, and honesty
expectations for supplied sources and evidence notes.

## Applies To

- skill:research
- workflow:research-topic-to-brief

## Scope

### Includes

- Source records, in-brief references, locators, missing metadata, and citation
  consistency.

### Excludes

- Mandatory use of one publication style and automatic bibliography lookup.

## Guidance

Assign each supplied source a stable source identifier. Preserve, when supplied:
author or organization, title, source type or publisher, publication date,
version, URL or repository location, access information, and provenance.
Evidence notes additionally require a stable note identifier and a locator such
as page, section, timestamp, table, row, or paragraph.

Use one consistent citation style within a brief. Put a citation adjacent to
each material claim and include a source record in the citations section.
Represent missing metadata as missing; never infer an author, date, title, URL,
or locator. Cite secondary discussion as secondary rather than implying access
to its primary source.

## Decision Rules

1. A citation must resolve to exactly one supplied source record.
2. A material claim needs a precise locator when the supplied record supports
   one.
3. Do not cite a source that was mentioned but not supplied as if reviewed.
4. Mark inaccessible originals and derivative citations explicitly.
5. Citation completeness and source reliability are separate assessments.

## Examples

### Good Example

Claim C1 cites `[S1, section 3; E1]`; the citations section preserves S1's
supplied title, date, type, and provenance.

### Counterexample

The brief creates a plausible URL and publication date for a note described
only as “a recent study.”

## Limitations and Risks

- A complete citation does not prove source authenticity or claim truth.
- Publication-specific citation formatting may require a downstream formatter.
