## Summary

Defines the required sections and traceability rules for a decision-useful
Research v1 brief.

## Applies To

- skill:research
- workflow:research-topic-to-brief

## Scope

### Includes

- Research objectives, scope, questions, requirements, findings, evidence maps,
  uncertainty, gaps, citations, and next steps.

### Excludes

- External retrieval, live browsing, academic peer-review procedure, and
  domain-specific legal, medical, or financial review.

## Guidance

A brief must contain:

1. **Objective and scope:** intended decision, audience, inclusions, exclusions,
   period, population, and definitions that materially affect interpretation.
2. **Research questions:** prioritized, answerable questions whose answers
   collectively address the objective.
3. **Source requirements:** suitable source types, authority, recency,
   independence, diversity, and coverage for each question.
4. **Findings:** concise answers supported by the claim-evidence map. Separate
   observations, source assertions, and analyst inference.
5. **Claim-evidence map:** each material claim, its support status, evidence
   note identifiers, source identifiers, contrary evidence, and confidence.
6. **Uncertainties and gaps:** what is unclear or absent, why it matters, and
   what evidence would resolve it.
7. **Citations and next steps:** traceable source records and prioritized
   follow-up work.

## Decision Rules

1. If no evidence is supplied, produce questions, requirements, gaps, and next
   steps, but no factual findings.
2. If evidence answers only part of a question, narrow the finding rather than
   broadening the evidence.
3. Put decision-relevant disagreement in findings as well as limitations.
4. Order next steps by decision impact and feasibility.

## Examples

### Good Example

A brief states that one internal retrospective supports a bounded observation,
labels generalizability unknown, and requests independent evidence before a
broader decision.

### Counterexample

A brief gives a confident recommendation followed by an unstructured source
list with no mapping from evidence to claims.

## Limitations and Risks

- Structural completeness does not establish factual correctness.
- High-stakes briefs require qualified domain review.
